essay: >-
  Public Oddities is a documentary series about the small comedy of moving through a city. Inspired by Martin Parr, I photographed everyday public spaces where people, signs, vehicles, trash, reflections, and architecture accidentally arrange themselves into strange little jokes. The theme is not a single dramatic event, but the way ordinary urban life becomes absurd when it is observed closely: a trash can beside a road sign, a person swallowed by a huge bus window reflection, commuters waiting under bright station light, or a lone pedestrian reduced to a pattern from above.

  My approach was observational and candid. I did not stage the scenes; I walked through streets, stops, sidewalks, and transit spaces, looking for moments where people were absorbed in their own routines. I kept a respectful distance and photographed public behavior without interrupting it. I used a mix of eye-level street views, low angles near cracked pavement, high angles from windows or bridges, close details, medium pedestrian scenes, and wider environmental frames. The series includes individuals, groups, public interactions, and the objects and settings that shape them, so the city itself becomes part of the subject.

  The editing stays documentary but intentionally bright and warm. I pushed color, contrast, and golden tones enough to create the visual energy associated with Parr, while keeping the imperfections visible: cluttered edges, tilted lines, blocked sightlines, motion blur, glare, and awkward spacing. Those flaws are the point. They make the photos feel less polished and more honest, showing contemporary life as funny, crowded, colorful, and slightly out of control.
